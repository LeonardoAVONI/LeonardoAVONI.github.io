Master Course Project · EPFL · 2022

Supersonic Wind Tunnel
Design & Testing

Design, manufacturing and experimental testing of a small-scale supersonic wind tunnel — from nozzle geometry to shock visualisation.

Overview

As part of EPFL's experimental aerodynamics course, our team designed and built a small-scale supersonic wind tunnel capable of reaching Mach numbers between 1.5 and 2.5 in the test section. The project covered the full engineering cycle: aerodynamic design, structural sizing, manufacturing, instrumentation, and experimental validation.

Design Phase

Nozzle Geometry

Method of Characteristics (MoC) was used to design the convergent-divergent (de Laval) nozzle contour that produces a uniform supersonic flow at the desired Mach number. The area ratio A*/A was computed for each target Mach number, and the nozzle wall profiles were generated numerically.

Structural & Sealing Design

The tunnel body was designed to withstand internal pressure differentials. Optical access windows (acrylic) were sized and sealed for schlieren visualisation. All parts were manufactured using CNC machining and 3D printing.

Experimental Testing

Pitot-Static Measurements

Mach number verification using pitot-static probes and Rayleigh Pitot formula for supersonic flow. Comparison against design targets.

Schlieren Visualisation

Z-type schlieren optical setup to visualise density gradients — shock waves, expansion fans, and boundary layers were made visible and photographed.

Shock Wave Analysis

Experimental oblique shock angles measured from schlieren images and compared with theoretical predictions from oblique shock relations.

Skills Demonstrated

Compressible flow theory · Method of Characteristics · Optical diagnostics · CAD/manufacturing · Experimental data analysis
